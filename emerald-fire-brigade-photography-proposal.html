Why I'm putting this on the table

I live in Belgrave. Your brigade is fifteen minutes away.

This is my home patch. The brigades across the Dandenongs are the reason families like mine sleep through a hot February. So when Jody rang and described the brief, the answer was an easy yes.

My standard fee for a body of work like this would be in the order of $5,000. The figures you see further down are a long way short of that, and intentionally so. This is my contribution to the Brigade. The photography needs to be done properly, and there is no reason it should come at full commercial rate when it is for the people who would turn up on the worst day of someone's life.

Two options are offered. The first covers the photography and digital delivery only. The second adds a printed portrait for every member and a framed group portrait sized to live on the station wall. Both come in within the spirit of what Jody described on the phone.

Why it matters for Emerald

The trap on a brigade shoot is the running order. Forty to fifty people, four group configurations, a training night happening in the background, and a portable studio that has to deliver consistent lighting across every frame. The work above is what teaches you how to keep all of that calm on the night.

What's covered on the night

Four group shots, every member's headshot, one evening.

Individual headshots first, as people arrive. The studio is set up before the first members walk in, so we can start photographing individuals as soon as the early arrivals are ready. Volunteers have lives outside the brigade and turn up at different times. Running headshots as a rolling check-in lets every member get through the queue without holding anyone up, and keeps the photography out of the way of training.

Group photos once everyone is there. Once the full brigade has gathered, we move to the group shots: the whole brigade with an appliance in the engine bay, then the Brigade Management Team, the Brigade Support Team, and the Female Members group. Same studio, same backdrop, same lighting for the sub-groups, so the full set reads as one body of work.

Digital delivery for publicity. The Brigade receives a full gallery of every group photo and every headshot, edited and at full resolution, with no licensing restrictions. Use them on the website, in the newsletter, in the local paper, on the recruitment posters, wherever they need to land.

A private online gallery for the members. Every member can browse their own headshot and the group photos through a private link and order any additional prints they would like, at their own cost, at standard print pricing. Nothing for the Brigade to administer.

How the night runs

Calm setup, calm shoot, calm pack down.

Early arrival to set up. I arrive ahead of training start with lights, backdrop, stands and rigging. Setup takes around 45 minutes. I will pick the spot for the portable studio on the day. All I need is roughly 4 metres of depth, 3 metres of width, a couple of metres of ceiling, and a power outlet within reach. The whole shoot is indoors with studio lighting, so the timing is not at the mercy of the weather or the time of year.

Individuals first, on a rolling basis. As soon as the studio is set, individual headshots begin. Members slot in as they arrive and leave the queue as soon as they are done. By the time the full brigade is on site, the individual portraits are largely behind us.

Group photos at the back end. Brigade with an appliance in the engine bay, Management Team, Support Team, and Female Members group. All shot once everyone is present, dressed, and ready.

Basic editing in post. Two to three weeks. Every selected image gets colour and contrast correction, applied consistently across the full set.

Digital delivery. Full gallery and downloads handed over by mid-August, well ahead of the 5 September Annual Dinner.

Prints (if Add-On A or Add-On B selected). Lab-printed and, in the framed case, framed and quality-checked by me before delivery. In the Brigade's hands by the first week of September at the latest.

Payment. Invoice issued on delivery of the digital gallery. No deposit required.

What I need from the Brigade to lock the date

Three quick things.

1. A preferred Wednesday between now and the end of July. Working back from the 5 September dinner, the shoot needs to be in the diary by end of July to leave time for editing and printing. Most Wednesdays across June and July are open at my end — name the one that suits the Brigade's calendar and I will work around it.

2. A point of contact on the night. Jody, happy for that to be you? Or whoever else is best placed to help shepherd members through the headshot queue while training and meetings are running.

3. A heads-up to members ahead of the night to come dressed for the occasion. Turnout uniform or whatever the Brigade considers appropriate for portraits that will hang on a wall and live on the website for years. The portraits are only as good as the dress code on the night.

Ready when the Brigade is.

Once the Brigade Management Team has chosen a tier and approved the proposal, drop me a line. I will put together a formal quote and a simple contract for sign-off, and we will lock the Wednesday.
