Sexual Harassment Prevention Policy and Plan

Applies to: employees, subcontractors, clients, talent and any third party we work with  ·  Version 1.0  ·  Last updated 17 June 2026
Five Castles Pty Ltd (ABN 76 651 385 545), trading as Fifth Castle Media

Our commitment

Sexual harassment is not tolerated at Fifth Castle Media in any form, by anyone, on any job. Everyone we work with has the right to feel safe and respected, including talent and clients we have only just met.

The law no longer waits for a complaint. We have a positive duty to take active steps to prevent sexual harassment before it happens. This document is both our policy, setting out what is not tolerated and how to report it, and our prevention plan, setting out the specific risks in our work and how we control them. It is written to meet our duties under the Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth), the Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic), the Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004 (Vic), and the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth).

Who and where this covers

This policy covers conduct by and towards everyone connected with our work: employees, subcontractors and their workers, clients, talent, and other third parties. It applies wherever work happens, including our own space, client premises, location shoots, travel, and work-related social settings.

What sexual harassment is

Sexual harassment is any unwelcome sexual advance, unwelcome request for sexual favours, or other unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature, in circumstances where a reasonable person would have anticipated the possibility that the person would be offended, humiliated or intimidated. It does not matter whether harm was intended. It is judged on the conduct and how a reasonable person would see it.

Sex-based harassment is unwelcome conduct that is demeaning and directed at a person because of their sex, even if it is not sexual.

A hostile environment is conduct that makes a workplace offensive, intimidating or humiliating for people of a particular sex, even when it is not aimed at any one person.

Victimisation is treating someone badly, or threatening to, because they have made or supported a complaint. It is prohibited and treated as seriously as the harassment itself.

What it can look like on our jobs

  • Unwelcome comments about someone's body or appearance, or sexual jokes and innuendo on set.
  • Staring, leering, or unnecessary or unwelcome touching.
  • Pressure of any kind during a one-on-one shoot.
  • Sexually explicit messages, images or requests.
  • Touching a person to fit equipment without explaining first and asking permission.

Close-contact work and fitting microphones

Fitting lapel microphones, and working one-on-one with talent, puts our crew in close contact with people who may have never been on a shoot before. We treat this as a known risk and manage it the same way on every job. This protocol protects talent and clients, and it protects our crew from situations being misread.

Microphone-fitting protocol

1. Explain first. Before going near anyone, introduce yourself, show the microphone, and explain where it needs to sit and how it clips on.

2. Ask permission to touch. Ask clearly and wait for a yes before any contact. For example, "Is it alright if I clip this to your collar?"

3. Offer self-fitting as the default. Most placements (collar, lapel, belt pack) can be fitted by the person themselves. Offer this first and give simple instructions. Only assist hands-on if they would prefer it and agree.

4. Have someone present. For any placement near the chest, waistband, or under clothing, have another person present. On a solo shoot, keep to over-clothing self-fitting, or bring a second person.

5. Keep contact minimal and narrated. Touch only what is necessary, say what you are doing as you do it, and stop immediately if asked.

6. Respect privacy and comfort. Fit in a suitable, reasonably private space, not in front of an audience, and offer an alternative to anyone who is uncomfortable.

Working alone with talent

Avoid one-on-one situations where the job allows it. Where it cannot be avoided, log the location and timing, keep a check-in arrangement, prefer visible and accessible spaces, and make sure talent has an easy way to pause or stop at any time. Where practicable, brief talent and clients on this protocol at the start, so the close contact is understood as professional and consented to.

How to report

You can raise a concern informally or make a formal complaint. Either way, it will be taken seriously. You do not have to have all the facts, and you do not have to be the person it happened to.

Inside the business

Speak to Arek Rainczuk at info@fivecastles.com.au or on 0467 676 205.

Outside the business

You can always go to an external body instead of, or as well as, raising it with us. This matters in particular if a complaint involves Arek, in which case the external options below are the appropriate path.

  • Fair Work Commission (can make orders to stop sexual harassment): fwc.gov.au
  • Australian Human Rights Commission: humanrights.gov.au, 1300 656 419
  • Victorian Equal Opportunity and Human Rights Commission: humanrights.vic.gov.au, 1300 292 153
  • WorkSafe Victoria (sexual harassment as a work health and safety matter): 13 23 60
  • Victoria Police: for any conduct that may be a crime, call 000 in an emergency, or 131 444.

How we respond

We respond promptly, fairly and confidentially. We listen to the person affected and respect their wishes about how to proceed wherever we can. Where a matter needs to be looked into, both the person who raised it and the person it concerns are treated fairly and given a chance to be heard. Outcomes depend on what is found and can range from a conversation through to ending an engagement or employment, and reporting to police where a crime may have occurred.

Confidentiality

Information about a complaint is kept confidential and shared only with those who need to know to deal with it properly or as the law requires.

No victimisation

No one will be disadvantaged for raising a concern in good faith, supporting someone who has, or taking part in a process. Victimising someone for doing so is itself a breach of this policy and the law.

Support

Support is available to anyone affected, whether or not a formal complaint is made. We will help you access support, including external counselling services, and give you time and space to do so.

Responsibilities

Arek is responsible for leading on this, keeping the policy and plan current, acting on risks and reports, and making sure everyone knows about it. Everyone on our jobs is responsible for behaving respectfully, following the protocols above, and speaking up if they see or experience a problem. Subcontractors agree to uphold this policy as a condition of engagement, and we ask the same standard of conduct from clients on our jobs.

Our prevention plan

This is how we identify and control the risk, not just state our position on it.

Risks we have identified in our work

  • Fitting microphones and other close-contact work on talent.
  • Working one-on-one or alone with talent, sometimes in private or after-hours locations.
  • Filming inside client workplaces, where power imbalances and unfamiliar people are present.

How we control them

  • The microphone-fitting protocol and lone-work rules above, used on every job.
  • Briefing talent and clients on the protocol up front.
  • Clear, multiple reporting paths, including external ones.
  • Treating sexual harassment as a psychosocial hazard under our Work Health and Safety Policy.

Consultation and review

We consult the people doing the work when we set and review these controls. This plan is reviewed at least once a year, and sooner if an incident or a change in our work or the law calls for it.
