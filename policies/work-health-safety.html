Work Health and Safety Policy

Applies to: employees and subcontractors  ·  Version 1.0  ·  Last updated 17 June 2026
Five Castles Pty Ltd (ABN 76 651 385 545), trading as Fifth Castle Media

Our commitment

Fifth Castle Media is committed to providing and maintaining a working environment that is safe and without risks to health, so far as is reasonably practicable. This applies on every job, whether in our own space, at a client site, or on location.

We operate in Victoria, so our duties come from the Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004 (Vic) and the Occupational Health and Safety Regulations 2017 (Vic), regulated by WorkSafe Victoria. The word "OHS" is used through this policy because that is the Victorian term, but it means the same thing as "WHS".

Safety is not separate from good production. A calm, well-planned shoot is also the safest one. We expect everyone on our jobs to take it seriously.

Who this applies to

This policy applies to everyone who works on a Fifth Castle Media job: the director, employees, and subcontractors and their workers. It also covers the safety of clients, talent and members of the public who are present while we work. Our duty of care extends to subcontractors for the parts of a job we control, and that responsibility cannot be signed away.

Responsibilities

The business and the director (Arek)

  • Plan jobs so that hazards are identified and controlled before work starts.
  • Provide safe equipment, safe systems of work, and the information, instruction and supervision people need to work safely.
  • Consult the people doing the work about safety, and act on what they raise.
  • Make sure incidents are reported, recorded and, where required, notified to WorkSafe.

Everyone on the job

  • Take reasonable care for your own safety and the safety of others around you.
  • Follow the safe systems of work and any site-specific rules.
  • Use equipment properly and report anything that is damaged or unsafe.
  • Speak up early. If something does not feel safe, stop and raise it. No one will be penalised for stopping unsafe work.

How we manage risk

Before and during a job we identify the hazards, assess the risk, and control it. We control risk in order of effectiveness: remove the hazard if we can; if we cannot, reduce it through a safer method, equipment, or as a last resort, instruction and protective gear. "Reasonably practicable" means we weigh how likely harm is and how serious it could be against what it takes to control it. Cost is considered last and never excuses leaving an obvious risk in place.

The hazards on our work, and how we control them

Manual handling

Camera, lighting and grip equipment is heavy and awkward. Use trolleys and cases with wheels, plan team lifts for anything heavy, keep loads close to the body, and do not lift beyond a safe limit on your own.

Electrical

Lighting and power can injure or kill. Use leads and equipment that are in good condition and tested where required, do not overload circuits, keep cables clear of water, and only competent people rig and power lighting.

Working at heights

Avoid working at height where the shot allows it. Where it cannot be avoided, use the right access equipment, never work at height alone, and do not improvise with furniture or unstable supports.

Slips, trips and cables

Cables are the most common trip hazard on a set. Run them along edges, tape or mat runs across walkways, and keep access paths clear.

Fatigue and long days

Shoot days run long. Plan realistic schedules with breaks, and do not let people drive home fatigued after a long shoot. Fatigue is both a physical and a psychological hazard.

Driving

Travel to and from locations is part of the job. Drive to the conditions, secure loads properly, and never drive while fatigued or otherwise unfit.

Working alone or in remote locations

Where a crew member is working alone or one-on-one with talent, agree a check-in arrangement, share the location and timing, and prefer visible, accessible spaces.

Close-contact work, including fitting microphones

Fitting lapel microphones and similar close-contact work is a hazard we manage deliberately, for the safety and dignity of both talent and crew.

See also

The full protocol for fitting microphones and for working alone with talent is set out in the Sexual Harassment Prevention Policy and Plan. It is a required control on every job, not optional.

Psychological health

Health includes psychological health. Under the Occupational Health and Safety (Psychological Health) Regulations 2025 (Vic), we identify and control psychosocial hazards just as we do physical ones. For our work these include long hours and fatigue, lone and isolated work, exposure to distressing content while filming or editing, and sexual harassment. We manage these through realistic scheduling, check-ins, the close-contact protocol, and a clear, safe way to raise concerns.

If something goes wrong

Report every incident, injury or near miss to Arek as soon as it is safe to do so, however minor it seems. We record what happened so we can stop it happening again.

Serious (notifiable) incidents

Some incidents must be reported to WorkSafe Victoria. These include any death, a serious injury or illness requiring hospital treatment, electric shock, a fall from height, and other serious events listed in section 38 of the OHS Act. If one occurs:

1. Make the area safe and care for anyone injured.
2. Call WorkSafe Victoria immediately on 13 23 60.
3. Do not disturb the site until an inspector allows it, except to help someone or make it safe.
4. Tell Arek straight away. Written notice follows within 48 hours and the record is kept for at least five years.

Emergencies

On arrival at any location, know where the exits, first aid and assembly point are. In an emergency, people come first: stop work, get everyone to safety, and call 000 if needed. Equipment can be replaced.

Training and induction

Everyone is inducted into how we work safely before their first job. On client and construction sites, complete the site induction as well, and follow the site rules in addition to ours.

Subcontractors

As a condition of engagement, subcontractors must work safely, follow this policy and any site rules, hold their own current public liability insurance, and provide a safe work method statement for higher-risk work (such as working at height, rigging, electrical work, or drone operations) where we or the client require one. Verified competency and licences must be held for any work that requires them.

Review

This policy is reviewed at least once a year, and sooner if our work changes, an incident points to a gap, or the law changes.
