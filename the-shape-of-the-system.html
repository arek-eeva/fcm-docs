The frame

A hire, not an install.

Think of it as a sharp colleague who's brilliant from day one and blank every morning. What would you do for that hire? Write everything down and leave it on their desk.

That's all the system is. Plain files it reads at the start of every session, so it never turns up with amnesia again. If you can write it down, it can act on it.

The architecture

Brain, memory, hands.

A handful of folders is the whole thing, in plain English. You can open and read every file, and they live on your own machine.

The architecture

Two speeds of truth.

What the business is
Stable · changes rarely
  • Pricing
  • Values
  • Brand voice
  • How you work
lives in → the brief, brand/
What's happening now
Live · changes daily
  • Live jobs
  • This week's focus
  • Money owed
  • Today's decisions
lives in → trackers, logs, a current note

Mix them and the brief goes stale while you re-explain your own business every morning. Keep them apart and it stays true while the day moves.

Why files win

Change it once. It changes everywhere.

Because it works from your files, not a one-off chat, one change carries through everything that follows. Change your price and your proposals, your site copy and your email templates all carry it from that moment. A decision made is a decision implemented.

A chat window can't do that. It forgot you overnight.

For the first month, start everything here, even when it feels slower. You're not saving time yet. You're teaching it the boring parts, so you never have to do them again.

The habit

Write it down once.

1
The rule of three. Do something three times, write it down. It's a playbook now, and you never start it from scratch again.
2
Procedures, not prompts. "Do a proposal for Sarah" runs your whole way of doing proposals, not a fresh ask each time.
3
A head start. A few come ready-made when you begin. You rewrite them in your own voice.
Under the hood

One you talk to. A team behind it.

You deal with one assistant. Behind it, it puts a small team of helpers to work and checks their output before it reaches you. One builds, one reviews. You see the finished result, not the machinery.

The first hour

You don't start from scratch.

The architecture is already built, roughly 80% of it, before you arrive. In the first hour you say "let's begin", it reads your website, folds in your old AI chats, and asks a few good questions. Within ten minutes it knows most of your business.

You're not building from zero. You're making a working system yours.

The point of it

The breakthrough isn't a feature. It's the first thing you'd actually send.

A quote, a brief, an email that's good enough to go out, in your voice, in the first sitting. That's the moment it clicks, and the moment you see you can run and extend this yourself.

You don't need to be technical to get there. If you can write things down, you can run this.

Start here

Three questions to start with.

1
What do you find yourself re-explaining most often? That's your brief.
2
What do you do three or more times a month? That's your first playbook.
3
What would you hand over tomorrow, if you trusted it? That's your first win.
