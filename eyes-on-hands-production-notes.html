Section 01

Project Overview

A safety training video for CivilMart workers demonstrating 10 real hand injury scenarios and correct preventative behaviour. Based on actual incident data from 2022 to 2026. The finished film is one master training video of five to ten minutes, with all 10 scenarios presented in sequence.

Tone target: 8 out of 10 on the confronting scale. Visceral impact, not gratuitously graphic. The purpose is to stay with workers long after they leave the room.

Core Concept

The 7-Beat Structure — Repeated Across All 10 Scenarios

01
The Wider Situation
Normal workplace activity. Everything looks routine.
02
The Victim
A worker doing a routine task. Familiar. Focused. Not careless.
03
The Mistake
Hand goes to the wrong place. Close-up. The moment before impact.
04
The Accident
AI-generated injury extension. 8 out of 10 on the confronting scale. The consequence is real.
05
The Rewind
Footage rewinds back to the critical moment. A second chance.
06
The Gesture
A coworker taps eyes, points at hands. Simple. Clear. No words needed.
07
The Safe Correction
Worker moves hand to the safe position. Scene resolves. Life continues.
"If you're touching it, you're risking it. Hands off. Eyes on hands."

The 10 Scenarios

1
Rolling Pipe
IR1100 MTI Shepparton Pipes Afternoon Session
What Happened

Operator completing patching process on a pipe. Rolling pipes on the bench, not quick enough to move hands. Hand crushed between the pipe being rolled and the adjacent pipe.

Safe Method

Step away from the table while pipes are being placed. Maintain the exclusion zone from the forklift and its load at all times.

Shot List

  • WideForklift approaching pipe storage area. Picks up a large concrete pipe from the stack.
  • TrackingForklift driving through the yard with the pipe, weaving between work areas. Shows the weight and scale of the load.
  • MediumPatching worker at the far end of the table. Focused on his task, head down. Comfortable rhythm. Done this every day.
  • Close-upWorker's hands on the table surface. Patching detail. Skilled, routine hand movements.
  • WideOver-forklift angle. Forklift approaching the patching table from the opposite end. Worker visible in the background, unaware.
  • MediumForklift operator placing the pipe onto the table. Concentrating on his placement.
  • Close-upTable surface tilting as the weight shifts. Subtle but visible movement.
  • Close-upPipe beginning to roll along the tilted table. Slow. Inevitable.
  • Close-upWorker's hands still flat on the table. The pipe rolling toward them. The danger.
  • MediumWorker glances up. Recognition. But too late.
  • AIPipe rolls into the worker's hands. Crush impact.
  • RewindFootage reverses to the moment before the forklift places the pipe. Freeze on the worker's hands flat on the table.
  • WideA worker walking nearby, carrying something, going about his business. He notices the forklift approaching the patching table.
  • MediumPasserby's eyeline. He looks at the forklift, then at the patching worker's hands on the table. He sees the situation developing.
  • Close-upPasserby pauses. A beat of recognition. He makes the decision to act.
  • Close-upPasserby taps his own eyes with two fingers. Deliberate. Clear.
  • Close-upPasserby points directly at the patching worker's hands. The gesture.
  • MediumTwo-shot. Both workers in frame. The gesture connecting them.
  • MediumWorker steps back from the table. Hands clear. Forklift places the pipe. Table tilts. Pipe shifts. Nobody near it.
  • WideWorker waits. Steps back in. Continues patching. Both carry on with their day. Resolved.
Production Notes
Requires the pipe patching table and a forklift with a real pipe. The roll needs to look natural on camera — stage it with Thomas first. The table tilt should be subtle but clearly visible. Thomas has a "hands poster" related to this incident; review it before filming for reference.

Lifting a Grate by Hand
IR2906 LTI — Surgery Shepparton Precast Morning Session
What Happened

Worker tried to straighten a 54 kg galvanised metal grate sitting on top of another. The underside has extended fins. When he grasped underneath, the fins fell through the gaps in the lower grate, crushing his left middle finger. Surgery required.

Safe Method

Use an approved mechanical lifting aid. Glenn confirmed: chains with gantry, clamp, magnet, or designated cover lifters. Reference "Woodsy's" section — stack grates the right way, roll correctly, then use chains.

Shot List
  • WideGrate storage area at Shepparton Precast. Stacked heavy steel grates, industrial scale, workers in the background.
  • MediumWorkers moving product around. Normal production rhythm. Busy.
  • MediumWorker walks over to a stack of two grates. The top one is sitting crooked.
  • Close-upWorker's face. Assessing the situation. Making a quick decision.
  • WideThe lifting aid or gantry is visible nearby, maybe 10 metres away. He glances at it. Decides not to bother.
  • MediumWorker crouches down beside the grates. No tools. Just bare hands.
  • Close-upUnderside of the top grate. Extended fins visible. Dangerous geometry.
  • Close-upGaps in the lower grate. The trap is set.
  • Close-upWorker's hands reaching under the top grate. Fingers threading between the fins.
  • XCUFinger positioned directly above a gap in the lower grate. The pinch point.
  • Close-upWorker lifts and shifts. The fin slips through the gap onto his left middle finger.
  • AICrush. Fin drops through gap onto finger.
  • RewindBack to hands going under the grate. Freeze. Fins, gaps, fingers aligned.
  • MediumAnother worker nearby, carrying equipment, sees the first worker crouching beside the grates.
  • Close-upPasserby's face. Recognition. He sees the hands going under. Puts down what he is carrying.
  • Close-upPasserby taps his own eyes. The signal.
  • Close-upPasserby points at the hands under the grate.
  • MediumTwo-shot. The gesture lands. Worker stops. Looks at his own hands.
  • MediumWorker stands up. Walks to the gantry. Attaches the lifting aid to the grate.
  • WideMechanical repositioning. Hands clear of the underside the entire time. Worker straightens up, walks away. Done properly.
Production Notes
Need the actual grate type (galvanised, finned underside, approximately 54 kg) and the correct lifting aid used on site. The "decides to do it himself" moment is the key beat — make the lifting aid visible in the background while he walks past it. LTI with surgery: AI extension should reflect the severity.

Placing Timber Under a Rebar Bundle
IR1501 MTI Shepparton Precast Morning Session
What Happened

While a 16 mm x 12 m rebar bundle was being lifted by forklift, a worker placed timbers underneath. The tie wire strap holding the bundle snapped, releasing the bars, crushing his right pinky finger between two timbers.

Safe Method

Sling the bundle properly. Do not rely on tie wire for load security. Timbers are placed only once all hands are confirmed clear of the drop zone.

Shot List

  • MediumForklift approaches a stack of rebar bundles. Picks up a 16 mm x 12 m bundle.

  • MediumForklift holds the bundle in position. Worker crouches and starts sliding timbers underneath.
  • Close-upWorker's right hand between the timbers, positioning them. Practised movement.
  • Close-upTie wire strap under increasing tension. Straining. The hidden danger.
  • Close-upWorker's hand is between timbers, directly under the load. Totally exposed.
  • Close-upWire snaps. A sharp release. Bars shift and spread.
  • AIBars fall. Right pinky crushed between two timbers.
  • RewindBack to the moment hand goes between timbers. Freeze. Wire visible, under strain, hand in the crush zone.
  • WideA worker walking past the area. Coming from another task. Normal movement.
  • MediumPasserby sees the hand going under the suspended bundle. He stops walking.
  • Close-upPasserby's reaction. Concern, then decision.
  • Close-upTaps eyes.
  • Close-upPoints at the hands between the timbers.
  • MediumTwo-shot. Gesture connects. Worker looks up.
  • MediumWorker pulls hand clear. Sling wrapped around the bundle. Forklift lifts properly.
  • WideTimbers placed while hands completely clear. Bundle set down. Both workers walk off.
Production Notes
Need forklift, rebar bundle, timbers, tie wire, and sling. The tie wire snap is the dramatic centre of the scene — stage it safely and convincingly. The body language matters: this worker has done this a hundred times. He is not reckless. That is the point.

Holding Rebar During MIG Welding
IR2486 LTI — Hospital & Surgery Shepparton Precast Morning Session
What Happened

Left-handed worker tack welding rebar into a precast cage. MIG wire pierced the welding glove and punctured his right index finger. He did not report it until the next day. Hospital admission and surgery to flush debris from the wound.

Use a clamp or holding tool to secure the piece being welded. Hands stay completely out of the weld zone. Gloves are a last line of defence, not a solution.

Cleaning a Kibble Jaw by Hand
IR3263 MTI Shepparton Precast Morning Session
What Happened

Worker clearing clogged concrete from a skip (kibble) jaw by hand. A second worker at a remote control station operated the jaw, closing it on the first worker's right middle finger. Laceration resulted.

Safe Method

Use a long-handled tool such as a jimmy bar to clear the jaw. The kibble has no proper lockout isolation point. The safe behaviour is hands off, tool in. Confirm verbally with remote operator before approaching.

Shot List
  • WideConcrete batching and pouring area. Skip (kibble) hanging from crane or sitting on a frame. Active production.
  • MediumWorkers at different stations. Concrete production in progress. Busy site.
  • MediumWorker A notices the skip jaw is clogged with dried concrete. Walks up and assesses.
  • Close-upThe clogged jaw. Concrete build-up visible. The blockage is the problem.
  • Close-upWorker A's hands. No tools. He reaches in and starts picking at the concrete.
  • MediumWorker A, hand inside the jaw opening, clearing it quickly. Just a minute's work.
  • WideCut to Worker B at the remote control station. Separate location. Out of Worker A's line of sight entirely.
  • Close-upWorker B at the remote. Ready to operate the skip. Does not know anyone is near the jaw.
  • Close-upWorker A's fingers deep inside the jaw opening.
  • Close-upWorker B presses the button. The jaw starts to close.
  • AIJaw closes on fingers. Laceration to right middle finger.
  • RewindTwo frames shown: Worker A's hand inside the jaw, and Worker B at the remote, unable to see Worker A. The disconnect.
  • MediumWorker B, about to press the remote, pauses. Looks toward the skip first.
  • Close-upWorker B's eyeline. He can see movement near the jaw.
  • Close-upWorker B puts the remote down. Walks over.
  • Close-upTaps eyes.
  • Close-upPoints at the hand in the jaw.
  • MediumTwo-shot. Worker A steps away from the jaw.
  • MediumWorker A grabs a jimmy bar. Long-handled scraping tool. Clears the concrete with hands well clear of the opening.
  • WideBoth workers confirm verbally before operating the skip again. Skip operates. Both walk away.
Production Notes
Requires concrete skip with an operable jaw and remote control. Two actors. The physical separation between the two workers is the core danger — frame it so the audience understands neither can see the other. Do not show lockout tag-out as the solution here. Tool use is the message.

Reaching Into a Rebar Cutter
IR2525 LTI — Partial Amputation Shepparton Precast Morning Session
What Happened

Worker cutting five lengths of 12 mm rebar on a cutting machine. After the cut, he reached into the machine to remove the cut portions. The blade activated unexpectedly and amputated a portion of his right ring finger.

Safe Method

Isolate the machine before reaching in. Power off, lock out, confirm dead. Do not reach into any cutting machine while it has power, no matter how quick the retrieval seems.

Shot List
  • WideRebar processing area. Cutting and bending machines, stacks of rebar, production rhythm.
  • MediumWorker feeding 12 mm rebar into the cutting machine. Makes a cut, then another. Routine.
  • Close-upMachine in operation. Rhythmic cutting sounds. Efficient.
  • MediumWorker finishes his fifth cut. Cut pieces sitting inside the machine. They need clearing.
  • Close-upWorker pauses. Looks at the pieces inside the machine.
  • Close-upWorker's face. Quick calculation. Does not bother turning the machine off. It will only take a second.
  • Close-upMachine controls. Still powered. The humming has not changed.
  • Close-upWorker's hand entering the cutting zone. Fingers reaching for the cut pieces.
  • Close-upBlade mechanism. Still powered. Something triggers it. A sensor. A fault.
  • Close-upThe blade moves.
  • AIBlade contacts right ring finger. Partial amputation of the fingertip.
  • RewindBack to hand entering the machine. Freeze. Machine running. Hand inside. No isolation.
  • MediumWorker at the next machine. Looks across. Sees movement.
  • Close-upAdjacent worker's reaction. Alarm. He sees a hand going into a live machine.
  • Close-upShouts. Alert. Verbal warning.
  • Close-upTaps eyes.
  • Close-upPoints at the hand in the machine.
  • MediumWorker pulls hand back. Hits the power-off switch.
  • Close-upLock-out being applied. Machine confirmed dead. Blade does not move.
  • MediumWorker clears pieces from the isolated machine. Loads the next batch. Carries on. Properly.
Production Notes
This is the most severe incident in the series — partial amputation. AI extension needs impact, not exploitation. Sound Design Notes

Setup
Ambient site and factory sound. Natural. Busy. Nothing unusual.
Accident
Sharp audio cue: impact, crunch, or sudden silence. The sound must land before the AI visual does.
Rewind
Tape-rewind sound effect. Unmistakable. Signals the second chance.
Safe Correction
Calm resolution. Ambient sound returns to normal. Tension releases.

No music bed during scenarios. Music appears only in the opening statistics montage and the closing sequence. The silence after each accident is intentional — it should land on the audience. Do not fill it.

Narration: Voiceover runs through the full video. Factual and direct in tone, not dramatic. It states what happened, what went wrong, and what should have been done. The images carry the emotion.
