The brief

A film made for the room it will play in

First and foremost, this film is for the CRH board when they are in Melbourne in October. It is internal, not an ad, and it has to feel genuinely high-end, because the board puts a lot into these visits and will read Adbri partly through how it shows up in the room.

So it cannot just walk through the process: mine the limestone, ship it, make cement, ship it again. The board does not need the steps. What they want to feel is the scale of what Adbri owns, and why each part of the chain matters: why the quarry is worth holding, why the new vessel earns its keep, and why making cement here, the lowest-carbon way in the country, is hard for a competitor to copy.

It is also a very Australian story. Where CRH in Europe moves materials short distances by road, Adbri moves them by sea across half a continent: limestone from Klein Point to Birkenhead, then cement on to Port Melbourne, before the network spreads it across Victoria by road. That reach, and the fact that it is all one connected business, is really the point.

The approach

Value at every link

We would keep your locations and your order, but shift the film from describing a route to making a case. At each site it answers one plain question: why does this matter, and what is it worth? That is what keeps a board leaning in, and it lets the sustainability thread, the lower-carbon cement and the electric-capable vessel, land as the reason it is Adbri and not someone else. Four things make it feel like a board film.

Scale you can feel

The board will never stand at Klein Point or walk Birkenhead. Aerial work gives them the physical size they will never see in person, with reveals that pull back until the machinery is a speck against the operation.

Light and colour

From cold first light over the quarry to golden hour as the cement reaches Melbourne, and the colour travels too: the bare reds, greys and yellows of the South Australian country giving way to the greener tones of Victoria.

Texture and atmosphere

Real manufacturing is dust, steel and grit. We lean into it. Backlit dust in the morning sun, the dark interiors of an old plant cut by shafts of light. Authentic and high-end at once.

The human side

Genuine, unposed portraits of the people who make this happen: the pride they take in the work, the mateship between them, the real skill each stage demands. Every frame compliant.

Sound does as much work as the picture. Under a quiet bed of music, the real sound of each place comes through: gulls over the jetty as the lime loads, water against the hull of the silent ship, the roar of the fire in the kiln. Over the top, one of Australia's best male voiceover artists, an older, considered voice with real gravity, the kind a board settles into and trusts.

Beyond the boardroom

One shoot, designed to become many films

A board film that plays once and then disappears is money half spent. So we plan and shoot this one to keep earning long after October.

The same footage, recut with a new story and voiceover, becomes your EVP and recruitment film: the "reinvent the way the world is built" pitch to the people you want to hire. It can also feed a wider "who is CRH in Australia" piece, investor and town-hall content, and shorter cuts for social and web. We shoot with all of that in mind from the first day, in how we frame, how much we cover and how we keep it compliant, so you are not paying to reshoot later.

It goes further than the board film and the EVP, and working that out is part of the strategy up front. We sit down with you and map what else each site can give you, so while we are there we also gather footage for the videos you will want down the track: marketing, safety and induction, product and process, and internal communications. The board film always comes first and nothing on the day compromises it, but wherever there is a window, we use the access. When I said you would not need to film these sites again, I meant it.

To make that real, the project includes a Footage Library: every master clip, sorted and tagged by site, colour-managed, backed up and cleared for reuse. You end up owning something you can keep coming back to, not just one finished file.

The plan

Ten locations, two states, one tight schedule

  • Directed personally. I direct the film and am on site for the shoots, alongside a professional videographer and drone operator, with a professional photographer brought in where needed. The board film is led by the person who shaped the story, not handed down a chain.
  • Coverage, efficiently. Ten locations from quarry to customer across South Australia and Victoria. We pair sites into efficient days and use trusted local crews, so we are not flying gear and people around the country.
  • Compliance handled on site. A safety liaison on every shoot, to keep the crew safe and to make sure every frame is compliant: chin straps, sleeves, masks in the dusty areas, hair tied. Nothing the board sees will have a compliance problem.
  • The vessel, made easy. We film the Yampu loading and unloading; the in-transit footage you already hold is woven in, so there is no waiting on the ship's schedule.
  • Copyright, covered. Licensed music delivered with the certificate your legal team requires, as standard.
  • Approval, de-risked. A clear storyboard signed off up front, so the film that comes back is the film expected, then built to clear CRH review comfortably before October.
