01
The Opportunity

A cross-referral inside the world's largest building materials group

On Thursday 28 May at 10:01am, an email landed at info@fifthcastle.media. Georgia Allen at Adbri was looking for a videographer to film around twelve sites across South Australia and Victoria ahead of a CRH Board visit in October. She'd been given my details by Drew, the previous CEO of Civilmart, and Kirk Buckley, Civilmart's Head of Communications. A Zoom is booked for tomorrow.

Adbri is the cement, lime, concrete, masonry and aggregates arm of Australian construction materials. Headquartered in Adelaide, born out of Adelaide Brighton Cement (origin 1882), rebranded Adbri in 2022. Until July 2024 it was ASX-listed as a $2 billion business with a national footprint.

On 1 July 2024, CRH plc — the world's largest building materials business by revenue, NYSE and LSE listed — completed the acquisition of the 57% of Adbri it didn't already control alongside the Barro Group. Headline price: A$2.1 billion equity value. Adbri delisted. It became CRH's Australian cement and aggregates platform, sister-company to the Civilmart group of precast concrete businesses CRH had already built in Australia.

That's where the referral starts to matter. Kirk Buckley is the Head of Communications at Civilmart — the senior comms operator who decides who films Civilmart's work and aligns brand storytelling with the broader CRH Australia portfolio. He's the same Kirk that Civilmart's National HSEQ Manager, Glenn Ducat, has been working with on the Eyes on Hands safety video FCM is currently in post-production for. Kirk recommending FCM to his Adbri counterpart isn't a casual handoff. It's the comms-side gatekeeper inside one CRH Australia business pointing FCM at the comms-side gatekeeper inside another.

Drew is the previous CEO of Civilmart — the executive at the helm during FCM's biggest order years (2022–2024) and the "Drew's Update Videos" CEO monthly-update format we produced for him in 2023. As ex-CEO he carries peer-level credibility into Adbri's leadership conversations in a way no working-level referrer ever could.

Two referrals, two registers. The Head of Communications at the working level, and the previous CEO at the executive level. Adbri's Georgia almost certainly received Kirk's recommendation through her comms network and had Drew's name added as the executive-credibility chip. If FCM had been chasing CRH-Australia panel positioning explicitly, this is exactly the inbound that would signal the door had opened. We weren't chasing it. The door opened anyway.

Total billed with Civilmart since April 2022: approximately A$189,000 across 35 orders. Most recent CRH-group-level work: the CRH Brand Values Video Series (October 2025, A$38,830). Adbri is the natural next ring outward.

02
The Strategic Read

This isn't a corporate video. It's Adbri's pitch to the CRH Board.

The storyboard Georgia attached is titled "Rock to Market". The file she sent was mis-named "Road to market", but the document's actual title gives the whole thing away. The narrative spine is vertical integration as competitive moat.

The argument the film is making

We own the rock (Klein Point Quarry). We own the boat (Yampu, the world's first fully electric-capable limestone carrier). We own the kiln (Birkenhead, Australia's lowest embodied carbon GP cements). We own the distribution (Port Melbourne, Yarraville, ICL terminals). We own the downstream concrete and masonry network (Hy-Tec, Mawsons, Central Premix, Swan, BPS, Adbri Masonry). And we feed the rest of the CRH Australia portfolio (Civilmart).

The language is board language, not marketing language. "Strategic asset within Adbri's vertically integrated model." "Secures long-term resource supply." "Redefining how materials move." "Australia's lowest embodied carbon general-purpose cements." "Integrated logistics network." "Stable demand channels and greater value capture across the construction lifecycle." "Fully integrated network — creating value, resilience and supporting Australia's growth."

The XX placeholder statistics scattered through the storyboard are the giveaway. They're waiting for operational performance numbers — tonnes extracted, tonnes shipped, percentage emissions reduction, tonnes diverted from landfill. Operational performance numbers don't go in a recruitment video. They go in a Board paper.

Who the real client is: Georgia is the project lead. The real client is Adbri's leadership team preparing a value-add narrative for the CRH Board's October visit. CRH paid A$2.1 billion for Adbri eighteen months ago. The Board's October visit is when they evaluate the asset in person and start setting the next capex cycle. This film is the early proof-point that the deal was worth it.

What this implies for FCM's positioning:

  • Topline pricing pressure will be low. Story credibility and CRH-fit will be high. Board films don't get value-engineered.
  • The dual-use ask is real. The same footage will become the company overview for new-talent recruitment. That's not an afterthought, that's deliberate — it's how Adbri amortises this investment.
  • The Civilmart cameo in the storyboard is huge. One of the late scenes is "supply to Civilmart site". FCM has the Civilmart relationship. We can solve that scene ourselves without Adbri having to broker access. Small thing on paper, large thing in the room.
  • The downstream prize is panel positioning. If this lands well, FCM is now the trusted CRH Australia video supplier across at least two business units. That changes the next five years of FCM revenue mix.

04
Site Intel

What I should know before I walk into the call

Production complexity varies wildly across the storyboard — from "drone over a quarry" to "film aboard a working bulk carrier mid-voyage". Knowing each site's reality before the call lets me steer the conversation rather than absorb it.

South Australia cluster

Klein Point Quarry

Yorke Peninsula · 6.5 km south of Stansbury

Limestone quarry operating continuously since 1925, when the Adelaide Cement Company began extracting here to feed the Birkenhead plant. ~1.5 million tonnes per year of off-white fossiliferous Port Willunga Formation limestone, 85–92% carbonate content. A 30-metre-thick limestone unit beneath 3–4 metres of calcrete overburden.

The site has its own active jetty — built in the 1920s, extended in the 1960s with a T-section — which is where Yampu loads. The quarry-jetty pairing is what makes the vertical integration possible: no third-party logistics between rock and ship.

Production complexity Active quarry with daily blast schedules. Will need drone permissions, dust management, full PPE protocol, escort. ~2 hours from Adelaide via car ferry (Ardrossan).

Yampu (the vessel)

CSL-owned bulk carrier, operated for Adbri

The single most innovation-laden subject in the storyboard. Yampu is the world's first battery-powered, fully electric-capable self-unloading bulk carrier. 11,000 deadweight tonnes, purpose-built at Jiangjiang Nanyang Shipyard in China, named at ceremonies in March 2026. Will carry 2.7 million tonnes of limestone per year — a 35% lift on the vessel she replaces (Accolade II).

Initial mode: hybrid diesel-battery, replacing 25% of diesel and lowering Scope 1 emissions by 40% versus Accolade II. By 2028 the AYK Energy Aries+S battery system (131 tonnes, 6,758 kWh) is installed. Target 2031: fully electric operation, Scope 1 emissions under 10% of the predecessor. The name "Yampu" means dolphin in the Kaurna language of the Adelaide plains.

Owned by Canada's CSL Group; operated by CSL Australia for Adbri under a long-term contract.

Production complexity The hardest shot in the storyboard. Three options: drone-from-shore at Klein Point and Birkenhead (cheapest, weather-dependent), drone-from-escort-vessel mid-voyage (more expensive, weather-dependent, permits), or staged shots aboard during loading/unloading. Critical question for the call: Is Yampu in service yet? She was named two months ago, and the AYK battery system doesn't install until 2028. If the vessel isn't fully operational, this scene may need to be partially stock or composite.

Birkenhead Cement Plant

Port River, Port Adelaide

Adbri's flagship cement operation and the destination for every Yampu voyage. Uses refuse-derived fuel (RDF) supplied by a Veolia / ResourceCo partnership: over 1 million tonnes of RDF replacing natural gas, 1.48 million tonnes of waste diverted from landfill, an estimated 13,000 tonnes per year of greenhouse gas emissions avoided. The EPA SA approved the expanded RDF usage in 2023.

Adbri markets the output as "Australia's lowest embodied carbon general-purpose cements", a position the film leans into hard.

Production complexity Heavy industrial: heat, dust, noise, multiple discrete shooting locations inside the same complex (kiln, conveyors, mills, dispatch). FCM has filmed comparable Civilmart precast sites — Glenn Ducat's network. Background risk for me to track (do not raise on the call): there is documented community opposition in Port Adelaide to expanded plastics-heavy RDF use. The storyboard sells RDF as a clean win. If I end up writing or refining script copy, I'll stick to the verified emissions and landfill numbers and avoid overclaim.

Adbri Masonry — Ottoway

Blakeney Road / Grand Junction, Ottoway SA

Adbri's masonry head office and manufacturing, ten minutes from Birkenhead. Concrete bricks, blocks, pavers, retaining walls, reconstituted stone veneers. Includes the Brighton Masonry brand — exclusive to Bunnings — which is a recognisable Australian retail proof-point if the film wants to land a "you've seen this in your local store" moment for the recruitment cut.

Lineage: Besser blocks since 1957, formerly traded as Rocla Pavers and Masonry, Pioneer Building Products, Hanson and C&M Brick before rebranding to Adbri Masonry in 2009.

Production complexity Standard manufacturing site. Same SA trip as Birkenhead and Klein Point — one efficient three-day block.
Victoria cluster

Port Melbourne — ICL Terminal

Adbri / Barro Group cement distribution

Independent Cement and Lime is Adbri's exclusive cement distribution arm into Victoria and NSW — historically a 50:50 joint venture with the Barro Group, though one industry source suggests the JV structure may have shifted post-CRH (worth confirming). A new 45,000-tonne silo was commissioned at the Port Melbourne terminal in 2023. This is where bulk cement from Birkenhead arrives by coastal shipping and reaches the Victorian market.

Production complexity Active commercial port. Permits needed. Coordinate with the Yampu arrival schedule if we want the "ship arriving + unloading" shot to be authentic rather than staged.

South Wharf — Hy-Tec Concrete

Pier 32, 469–591 Lorimer Street, Port Melbourne

Hy-Tec is Adbri's national premix concrete brand — established 1989, joined the Adbri group in 2018, supplies more than 1 million cubic metres of concrete per year nationally. Notable Hy-Tec project in the same precinct: 20,000 cubic metres supplied to the Hilton Melbourne South Wharf hotel.

Production complexity Urban concrete plant. Filming during a real delivery cycle gives authenticity but coordination with despatch is essential.

Echuca — Mawsons Concrete

McMillan Road, Echuca · ~250 km north of Melbourne

Mawsons is Adbri's regional concrete and quarries play in Northern Victoria and Southern NSW — a 50:50 joint venture with the McCann family since 2007. Also trades as Milbrae and Country Concrete. Around 600 employees across 80 sites, 110+ year history. This is the "regional Australia" beat in the storyboard.

Production complexity 2.5-hour drive from Melbourne — overnight crew likely. Good drone country, regional aesthetic differentiates from the metropolitan Hy-Tec scene.

Yarraville — BPS Bagging Plant

Bagged Product Solutions · Western Melbourne

BPS is Adbri's bagged cement operation for retail and trade. The Yarraville plant has just been through a major modernisation: new automated mixer and weigher inside a renewed dry storage tower, a fully enclosed Z-conveyor to a new 17-metre processing structure, multi-level reclaim and packing systems. Dust and noise reduced, throughput improved.

The branded BPS trucks are part of the visual storyboard — the film wants to show the retail / trade end of the value chain.

Production complexity Newly modernised facility — will look great on camera. Standard industrial filming protocols.

The Call

What I'm trying to learn, what I'm trying to plant

Tomorrow's Zoom is discovery. The goal is to understand the political map inside Adbri, confirm the actual site list, and signal to Georgia that I think about this work the same way she does — without quoting a price.

About Georgia. Communications Consultant at Adbri, Central Coast NSW based. Came in via Fulton Hogan where she established the company's first communications function and was Group Manager, Communications — full remit across strategy, change management, internal comms, stakeholder engagement, media, brand and shareholder communications. She isn't a junior. She's a senior comms operator who has already managed acquisition-integration storytelling. Match her register.

A · Storyboard intent
  1. The storyboard tells a powerful vertical-integration story. Before we get into production, can you walk me through who wrote it and what conversations led to that structure? Surfaces the political map — who internally owns this.
  2. Who's the primary audience? You mentioned the CRH Board for the value-add cut and new talent for the overview. Is the Board visit a presentation, a site tour, or an offsite — when and how will they see this film? The viewing context shapes everything: pacing, length, music, voice.
  3. How long do you want each cut to run? Board cut likely 4–6 minutes. Overview likely 2–3.
  4. The placeholder stats — XX tonnes, XX% emissions — are these coming from Adbri's reporting team, or do you want help sourcing and verifying them? If they want help, that's a strategy line item.
  5. The script uses "we" throughout. Will it be voiced by an Adbri executive, a professional voiceover, or something else? Voice choice changes the whole tone — CEO VO feels like a Board paper; pro VO feels like brand film; mixed approach feels like a documentary.
B · Site list and logistics
  1. Can you share the full site list for the twelve locations? I want to map it against my crew capacity in SA and Vic. Confirms the gap between the storyboard's 9 sites and the "~12" figure.
  2. Is Yampu in service yet? She was named in March. The AYK battery system isn't installed until 2028. If she isn't operational, the transit shot becomes a planning problem.
  3. For the customer project, the Civilmart site, and the masonry TBC, who selects them? Any leaning towards specific sites yet? Opens the door to recommend Carrum Downs Cubis for the Civilmart scene.
  4. Who organises site access, PPE inductions, subject release forms, and any end-client permissions on the customer project site? Heavy industrial site access is the most underestimated production cost. Pin it down early.
  5. What's the delivery deadline? October Board visit suggests late September final cut — does Adbri need internal review rounds before then?
C · Strategic fit
  1. For the new-talent overview cut, what's Adbri's current talent narrative? Operational, trades, leadership, all of it? Signals whether they have a defined employer brand or are still building one.
  2. Are there must-show people — executives, plant managers, frontline workers — who'd carry weight in the Board cut?
  3. Is the CRH connection something to lean into more in the story, or is it deliberately understated? The current storyboard ties it in twice — the Civilmart shot and the closing "global scale" line.
D · Decision and budget — last, soft
  1. Who'll be giving the green light on final scope and budget — you alone, with a CFO sign-off, or does it route through CRH?
  2. Do you have a budget range you're working to, or is this brief still being shaped? I ask because that helps me tell you straight whether the storyboard as-drawn is realistic, or whether we'd need to trim or stage it. Permission-giving framing — not "what's your budget", but "let me give you honest feedback".

Where I want the mastermind to push me

The questions I'm bringing to the room

I've worked the brief and I know my own bias. The mastermind is where I want the contrary view — the things I'm not seeing, the moves I'm under-weighting, the risks I'm minimising because the deal is exciting.

Q1 · Pricing posture

Is A$80–130K the right band, or am I anchoring too low?

The band is built bottom-up from FCM's standard production costs. If I think of this from the buyer's direction — what an agency would charge a $2.1B parent for the same brief — the number could comfortably be A$150–250K. Am I pricing like a freelancer when I should be pricing like the strategic partner I am? Or is staying disciplined on cost the right way to secure the panel position for the long game?

Q2 · The Yampu question

How do I handle a hero shot of a vessel that may not be operational?

Yampu was named in March 2026. The AYK battery system isn't installed until 2028. The storyboard treats her as the centrepiece of the innovation narrative. If she isn't fully in service this year, the most marketable shot in the storyboard is either staged or stock. Do I flag this risk hard with Georgia on the call, or do I let it surface naturally once we get into logistics? Where's the line between honest counsel and creating doubt that costs me the deal?

Q3 · The Civilmart leverage

How heavily do I lean on the Civilmart relationship in the room?

It's the single strongest credibility chip I have. But I worry about two failure modes: (1) leaning so hard it sounds like a brag, which dampens the trust; (2) putting Drew or Kirk in an awkward middle by overplaying the internal-referral angle when their endorsement was supposed to be quiet. Naming Drew (ex-CEO) on the call is heavier than naming Kirk — ex-CEO referrals carry weight but also expectation. What's the right register? One mention and move on, or weave it through the conversation?

Q4 · The panel-positioning play

Should I be thinking about this as one project, or as the on-ramp to a CRH Australia retainer?

If I treat this as a project, I'll quote a project price and deliver well. If I treat it as the on-ramp, I might intentionally under-price, deliver beyond scope, and use this as the proof-piece that gets FCM onto the CRH Australia panel. The second move has higher long-term EV but burns capacity. CRH Australia probably spends well into seven figures a year on video across its businesses. Am I being too conservative by treating it transactionally?

Q5 · The capacity question

If this lands at the top of the band, can FCM actually deliver it well?

I'm a one-person business with a network of trusted contractors. A 6–8 shoot day, 12-site, dual-edit, board-facing film with a hard October deadline is operationally meaningful. The Civilmart base load doesn't pause. The Rogerson Kenny launch (week of 22 June, delivery 6 July) doesn't pause. The Down Syndrome Australia four-video block in October doesn't pause. Where's the realistic ceiling on what I can take on this year without quality slipping — and is Adbri the right project to expand capacity for, or is this where I should resist?

Q6 · The dual-use trap

Is "one shoot, two edits" a strength or a hidden margin killer?

It's the framing Adbri wants and it matches how I think. But two edits with different audiences (CRH Board vs new-talent recruitment) means two scripts, two structural builds, two rounds of feedback, two delivery cycles. The "efficiency" of shared footage often hides the doubled post-production cost. Should I be quoting these as two clearly priced edits up front, rather than letting them feel bundled?

Q7 · The Birkenhead RDF risk

If the script makes claims that don't hold up to scrutiny, what's my obligation?

Adbri sells the RDF story as a clean environmental win. The Veolia / ResourceCo emissions and landfill numbers are real, but there is documented community opposition in Port Adelaide around plastics-heavy RDF use. If I end up refining script copy and it overclaims, my name is on the production. Do I quietly stick to verified numbers and avoid overclaim? Do I raise the reputational risk proactively with Georgia? Or is this firmly Adbri's call and outside FCM's lane?
