Where I'm starting

Georgia, thank you for the storyboard. It is a strong, clear foundation, and it already does the hardest thing well: it is built around who the film is really for.

Rather than send back a line-by-line markup, I wanted to first share how I would approach this as a board-grade film, so you can see the direction before we work through the detail together. These are early thoughts, offered to open the conversation, and I am keen to hear yours.

The one idea

Value at every link, not a line on a map

On our call you said the thing that makes this film work. You want to move away from a step-by-step process and toward the value behind each part of the chain. That instinct is right, and it is what I would build the whole film around.

A board does not need the process explained to them. They need to feel the scale of what Adbri owns, and understand why each link, the quarry, the vessel, the plant, the reach into market, is an advantage a competitor cannot easily copy.

Same locations and the same order as your storyboard, but the film makes a case rather than describing a route. That is what holds a board's attention. It is also what lets the sustainability story, lower-carbon cement and the electric-capable vessel, land as the answer to why us, rather than as a list of features.

How it would feel

Four things that make it board-grade

01

Scale you can feel

The board will never stand at Klein Point or walk Birkenhead. The film has to give them the physical size they will never see in person. That is where aerial work earns its place: not a pretty flyover, but reveals that make the enormity land.

02

A story told in light

The film can travel from cold first light over the quarry, through the heat of the plant, to golden hour as the cement reaches Melbourne. Handled well, light alone carries rock to market without a word of voiceover doing the work.

03

Texture and atmosphere

Real manufacturing is dust, steel and grit. We lean into that rather than hide it. Backlit dust in the morning sun, the dark interiors of an old plant cut by shafts of light. It reads as authentic and high-end at the same time.

04

The human side

The board invests in its people, so we show them, and we show them compliant, framed against the scale of where they work. A few of these moments give the whole film a pulse.

A few moments I can already picture
i

First light breaking over the Klein Point quarry, dust hanging in the still morning air, the working face vast and quiet before the day begins.

ii

The vessel cutting across open water at dawn, seen from above, the single clearest image of the lower-carbon story.

iii

The close: the finished bridge, road or building that the rock became, the part of the story that stays in the world long after the board has moved on.

I am looking forward to this one. Whenever you are ready, I would welcome your thoughts.
