Briefing your Chief of Staff.

The gap between a frustrating chat and a Chief of Staff that nails the job is how you brief it. A few habits.

Give a brief, not a question

A chat answers a question and leaves the doing to you.

Your Chief of Staff takes the whole job: "Draft the follow-up to Sarah, she asked about the half-day, keep it warm and short." Your Chief of Staff writes it in your voice and files it where it belongs.

Context is the fuel

The more your Chief of Staff knows, the better the work. Your business is already in there. For a specific job, hand over the bits only you know: who it's for, what for, what good looks like.

Correct it once

Don't like something that came back? Say so plainly, and ask your Chief of Staff to remember it: "Never do that. Save it." That mistake won't happen again.

A chat forgets. Your Chief of Staff doesn't.

Be specific, and push back

Vague in, vague out. Say the specific, true thing.

If the answer is off, say so straight. Your Chief of Staff would rather be corrected than be politely wrong.

Talk, don't type

For anything longer than a line, use voice-to-text. Ramble. It's faster, and it captures more of you than careful typing does.

You're not writing prompts. You're briefing a capable colleague who already knows your business.
