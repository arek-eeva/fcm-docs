Driving Claude Code.

Claude Code is the desktop app your Chief of Staff lives in. The controls that matter sit in a row just below the chat box. Here's what each one does, and when to reach for it.

The mode button (below the chat box)

Three settings. Click the mode button below the chat box to change it, or tap Shift+Tab to cycle through them.

Default. Your Chief of Staff asks before it changes a file or runs anything. Reading and looking around is free. Good while you're finding your feet.

Plan first. Your Chief of Staff explores and writes out a plan, but changes nothing until you approve it. Reach for this on anything big or unfamiliar, you see the whole approach before a single file moves.

Auto-accept. Your Chief of Staff makes changes without stopping to ask each time. Use it once you trust the direction and want a long job to run without interruptions.

Rule of thumb: plan first for anything new or risky, auto-accept once you're confident, default in between.

Working with a plan (what plan-first mode gives you)

When you use plan-first mode, your Chief of Staff thinks it through and then opens the finished plan in its own panel, the whole approach laid out before anything happens. This is the cheapest moment to change direction, so read it.

Four choices sit at the bottom of the plan:

Reject. Not the right track. It starts over.

Revise. Tell it what to change and it re-plans, keeping what worked.

Accept. Good to go, but check in as it works.

Accept + auto. Go, and run the whole thing through without stopping to ask.

The surgical trick. Select any line in the plan to leave a comment right there, "do this differently," "drop this step." Far more precise than describing it from the top, and it's how you shape a big build into exactly what you want.

Choosing the model (the picker next to it)

Next to the mode button is the model picker. Three worth knowing, smartest to fastest:

Opus. The deep thinker. For hard judgement, careful writing, anything where getting it right matters. Slower, and it uses up your plan faster.

Sonnet. The balanced one. Quick and capable, the sensible default for most jobs.

Haiku. The sprinter. Fast and cheap, great for simple or bulk work like sorting files or quick look-ups. Less sharp on hard problems.

Your Chief of Staff chooses sensibly by default. For a big, careful piece, switch to Opus; for a long, simple grind, Haiku saves time and tokens. You can also just say "use Opus for this one."

Check it when you start. Whatever model you picked last tends to carry into your next session, so it quietly sticks. If answers feel oddly literal, forgetful, or it keeps losing track of what's connected, glance at the picker, you're most likely on Haiku. Bump it back up to Opus or Sonnet for anything that needs real thinking. A surprising amount of "why is it being dense" comes down to this one setting.

The context meter (below the chat box)

That little meter below the chat box shows how much working memory is left in this session. A long session fills it up.

When it's getting full, your Chief of Staff sums up the conversation so far and carries on, that's called compacting. You can ask for it yourself by typing /compact, or type /context to see what's taking up the room.

Nothing important is lost when this happens. Your real memory lives in your files; the meter is only the live conversation. When in doubt, start a fresh session and carry on.

Previewing changes

Before it changes a file, your Chief of Staff shows you exactly what it's about to do, the old and the new side by side, and waits for your okay (in default and plan-first mode). Read it, then approve or reject.

Changed your mind after the fact? Type /undo, or tap Esc twice, to rewind to before the change. Every edit can be undone.

Steering and feedback

Stop it. Tap Esc at any time. Your Chief of Staff halts and waits for you.

Correct it. Just say what's off, plainly: "too formal, warmer please." It takes the note and carries on.

Make it stick. For something it should never do again, tell your Chief of Staff to remember it: "Never use that phrase. Save that." From then on it holds across every future session, not just this one.

Report a bug. If the app itself misbehaves, type /feedback to tell the Claude Code team.

Private tokens (treat them like passwords)

Some tools connect with a private token, a long secret code such as an API key or an access key. It is a password. Anyone who has it can act as you, so guard it like one.

Never paste a token into the chat. The chat is saved, synced and sometimes screenshotted, so a token dropped there is exposed for good.

The safe way. Keep it in a private file your Chief of Staff can read but that never leaves your computer. Easiest path: ask your Chief of Staff to set it up. Say, "I need to give you my [X] key safely. Make me a secrets file that's kept out of backups, and tell me where to paste it." Your Chief of Staff creates the file, hides it from GitHub and from anything you publish, and you paste the key into the file, not the chat. From then on it reads the key from there and never prints it.

Safer still. Your computer's keychain (Mac Keychain or Windows Credential Manager) or a password manager. Your Chief of Staff fetches the key with a command when it's needed, so the raw code never sits in a file at all. Ask for this if you want the stronger option.

If one slips out. Pasted it in the chat, caught it in a screenshot, or backed it up by accident? Treat it as burned. Rotate it: go to the service, cancel the old key and generate a new one. It takes a minute, and the old key stops working straight away.

Don't try to memorise all this. The controls are right under the chat box when you need them, and your Chief of Staff will walk you through any of it.
