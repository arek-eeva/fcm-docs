Creative Approach

Scenario Structure

The video opens with hard statistics, then moves through 10 real incident scenarios. Each scenario is a self-contained 30–60 second mini-story that follows a consistent eight-beat pattern.

Pacing: Opening stats (60–90s), each scenario (30–60s), closing (30–45s). Estimated total runtime: 7–12 minutes.

Sound design: Ambient site sound during setup. Sharp audio cue at accident. Voiceover over black screen. Tape-rewind effect. Calm resolution. No music bed during scenarios. Music only in the opening montage and closing.

Narration: Voiceover plays over a black screen after each accident, between the AI-generated injury and the rewind. The viewer watches the accident, the screen fades to black, and the narrator speaks in the darkness. Factual and direct, not dramatic. States what happened and what went wrong. Then the footage rewinds and the safe correction plays out visually, without voiceover.

Scenario 1

Rolling Pipe

IR1100 MTI Shepparton Pipes (afternoon)

The Incident

A worker was completing the patching process on a pipe at one end of a table. A forklift brought a pipe from inside the shed and placed it on the table, bumping the pipes already there and causing them to move. A pipe rolled and crushed the worker's hand between the rolling pipe and the pipe he was patching.

The Safe Way

Step away from the table while pipes are being placed. Maintain an exclusion zone from the forklift and its load at all times.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide establishing shot of Shepparton Pipes yard. Scale of the operation, concrete pipes stacked high, workers and forklifts moving. A forklift picks up a large concrete pipe from inside the shed and brings it outside to the production area. Drives through the yard. Approaches the patching table. Site ambience. Forklift engine, beeping.
The Victim Worker at the far end of the patching table. Focused on his task, both hands on the pipe, completing the patching process. Comfortable rhythm. Done this every day. Doesn't look up. Forklift approaching from the other end. Patching sounds. Forklift getting louder.
The Mistake Over-forklift angle: approaching from opposite end. Worker visible in background, unaware. Forklift places the pipe, bumping existing pipes, causing them to move. Table surface shifts. A pipe begins to roll. Slow. Inevitable. Worker's hands still on the pipe he's patching. Glances up. Too late. Low rumble building.
The Accident AI extension: rolling pipe impacts the pipe he's patching, crushing his hand between the two pipes. Heavy impact. Silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "A pipe rolled down that table and crushed his hand between two pieces of concrete. He never looked up. Never heard it coming. The forklift was loading right behind him and he didn't step back."
Rewind Footage rewinds to before the forklift places the pipe. Worker's hands on the pipe, patching. Freeze. Rewind sound.
The Gesture Worker walking nearby sees the forklift approaching. Looks at the forklift, then at the patching worker's hands. Sees the situation developing. Pauses. Taps his own eyes with two fingers. Deliberate. Clear. Points directly at the hands. Two-shot: both workers in frame, the gesture connecting them. Short alert tone.
Safe Correction Patching worker steps back. Hands clear. Forklift places the pipe. Table shifts. Pipe moves. Nobody near it. Worker waits. Steps back in. Continues patching. Both carry on. Ambience returns to normal.

  • Wide: Forklift approaching pipe storage area. Picks up pipe from inside shed to outside.
  • Tracking: Forklift driving through the yard with the pipe. Driving in the production area.
  • Medium: Patching worker at far end of table. Focused, head down. Comfortable rhythm.
  • Close-up: Worker's hands on pipe. Patching detail. Both hands on the pipe.
  • Wide: Over-forklift angle. Approaching table from opposite end. Worker visible, unaware.
  • Medium: Forklift operator placing the pipe. Concentrating on placement.
  • Close-up: Table surface shifting as weight lands. Pipes bumping and moving.
  • Close-up: Pipe beginning to roll. Slow. Inevitable.
  • Close-up: Worker's hands still on pipe, patching. Rolling pipe approaching. The danger.
  • Medium: Worker glances up. Recognition. Too late.
  • Close-up: Passer-by taps eyes with two fingers. Deliberate. Clear.
  • Close-up: Passer-by points directly at patching worker's hands.

Scenario 2

Lifting a Grate by Hand

IR2906 LTI (Surgery) Shepparton Precast (morning)

The Incident

A worker tried to straighten a 54 kg galvanised metal grate on top of another. The underside has extended fins. When he grasped underneath, the fins fell through the gaps in the lower grate, crushing his left middle finger. Surgery required.

The Safe Way

Use an approved mechanical lifting aid. Glenn confirmed: chains with gantry, clamp, magnet, or designated cover lifters. Reference "Woodsy's" section for correct procedure.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide shot of grate storage area at Shepparton Precast. Stacked grates, heavy steel. Workers moving product around. Normal day. Factory ambience. Metal clanking.
The Victim Worker walks over to two stacked grates. Top one is crooked. Makes a quick decision. The forklift with jib attachment is visible nearby, maybe 10 metres away. He glances at it. Decides not to bother. Crouches down. No tools. Just bare hands. Footsteps. Fabric rustling.
The Mistake Close-up: hands reach under the top grate. We see the fins on the underside. We see the gaps in the lower grate. Fingers thread between the fins. He lifts and shifts. The fins slip through. Left middle finger is between them. Subtle tension tone building.
The Accident AI extension: fin drops through the gap onto the finger. Crush. Crunch. Silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "Fifty-four kilograms of galvanised steel, and he tried to shift it with his bare hands. The fin dropped through the gap and took his finger to surgery. The forklift with the jib was ten metres away."
Rewind Rewind to hands going under the grate. Freeze. Fins, gaps, fingers in the path. Rewind sound.
The Gesture Another worker nearby sees the crouch, sees the hands going under. Puts down what he's carrying. Taps eyes. Points at hands. Alert tone.
Safe Correction Worker stands up. Walks to the forklift with jib attachment. Uses the mechanical lifting aid to reposition the grate. Hands stay clear of the underside. Walks away. Done properly. Ambience returns to normal.

Shot List

  • Wide: Grate storage area, stacked grates, busy environment
  • Medium: Worker approaching crooked grate, assessing
  • Close-up: Fins on underside of grate (establish hidden hazard)
  • Close-up: Gaps in lower grate
  • Close-up: Hands reaching under, fingers threading between fins (the danger)
  • Close-up: "Eyes on hands" gesture
  • Wide: Forklift with jib attachment visible nearby while worker walks past it (the choice)
  • Medium: Worker retrieving forklift with jib attachment
  • Medium: Mechanical repositioning using lifting aid, hands clear
  • Wide: Worker walking away

Production Notes

  • Need the actual grate type (galvanised, finned underside, ~54 kg) and the correct lifting aid on site.
  • The "decides to do it himself" moment is key. The forklift with jib attachment must be visible in the background while he walks past it. Show the choice.
  • LTI with surgery. AI extension should reflect the severity.

Scenario 3

Placing Timber Under a Rebar Bundle

IR1501 MTI Shepparton Precast (morning)

The Incident

A forklift was lifting a 16mm x 12m rebar bundle. A worker was placing timbers underneath. The tie wire holding the bundle snapped, releasing the bars and crushing his right pinky between two timbers.

The Safe Way

Sling the bundle properly. Don't rely on tie wire. Place timbers when hands are clear of the load.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide shot of the rebar storage yard at Shepparton Precast. A forklift drives over to a stack of rebar bundles. Picks one up, a 16mm x 12m bundle held together with tie wire. Carries it across to the work area and holds it in position. Forklift engine. Metal shifting.
The Victim Worker waiting with timber dunnage. Forklift holds the bundle. Worker crouches down and starts sliding timbers underneath while the forklift holds the load. His right hand is between the timbers, positioning them. Routine. Done it a hundred times. Timber scraping on concrete.
The Mistake Close-up: the tie wire strap under tension. It's straining. The worker's hand is still between the timbers. The wire snaps. The bars shift and spread. Wire snap, a sharp ping.
The Accident AI extension: bars release and fall. Right pinky is crushed between two timbers. Metal clang, impact. Silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "The wire snapped. Twelve metres of rebar came loose and his hand was between the timbers underneath. A suspended load held together by tie wire. And he put his hands right under it."
Rewind Rewind to the moment the worker puts his hand between the timbers with the tie wire still holding. Freeze. The wire is visible, under strain. His hand is in the crush zone. Rewind sound.
The Gesture A worker walking past the area sees the hand going under the suspended bundle. Stops. Taps eyes. Points at the hands between the timbers. Alert tone.
Safe Correction The worker pulls his hand clear. A sling is wrapped around the bundle. The forklift lifts it properly. Timbers are placed while the worker's hands are completely clear of the load. The bundle is set down. Both workers walk off. Ambience returns to normal.

Shot List

  • Wide: Rebar storage yard, forklift picking up bundle
  • Tracking: Forklift carrying bundle to work area
  • Medium: Worker crouching with timbers, positioning underneath
  • Close-up: Tie wire strap under tension (the hidden danger)
  • Close-up: Hand between timbers (the danger moment)
  • Close-up: "Eyes on hands" gesture
  • Medium: Sling being wrapped around bundle
  • Wide: Proper lift, timbers placed, hands clear
  • Medium: Workers walking away

Production Notes

  • Need forklift, rebar bundle, timbers, tie wire, and sling.
  • The tie wire snap is the dramatic moment. Needs to be staged safely and convincingly.
  • The "done it a hundred times" body language is important. This is routine, not reckless. That's the point.

Scenario 4

Holding Rebar During MIG Welding

IR2486 LTI (Hospitalisation, Surgery) Shepparton Precast (morning)

The Incident

A left-handed worker was tack welding rebar into a precast cage. His right hand held the bar in position. The MIG wire pierced through his welding glove and punctured his right pointer finger. He didn't report it until the next day. Hospital admission, surgery to flush debris from the wound.

The Safe Way

Use a clamp or jig to hold the piece. Hands out of the weld zone entirely.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide shot of the precast cage assembly area. Rebar cages in various stages of construction. Welding sparks flying from a few stations. Workers moving around. Factory floor. Welding buzz. Metal on metal.
The Victim Worker at a partially completed rebar cage. Picks up a segment of rebar and positions it. Needs to hold it in place with one hand and weld with the other. Grabs the piece with his right hand (gloved), picks up the MIG welder with his left. Standard setup. Welding arc starting up.
The Mistake Close-up: his right hand grips the rebar, fingers close to the weld point. The MIG wire feeds automatically, glowing hot. Close-up: the wire tip moves toward the glove. It contacts. It pierces through the fabric. Welding buzz intensifying.
The Accident AI extension: the wire penetrates through the glove into the finger. Puncture. The worker pulls his hand back, but the damage is done. Welding cuts to silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "The MIG wire went through his glove and into his finger. He didn't say anything until the next day. By then he needed surgery to flush the debris out. He was holding the rebar with his bare hand in the weld zone. The jig was right there on the bench."
Rewind Rewind to the moment the worker grabs the rebar with his bare hand in the weld zone. Freeze. His hand, a glove, and nothing else between his finger and a hot wire. Rewind sound.
The Gesture A worker at the next cage over looks across. Sees the hand holding rebar right in the weld zone. Taps eyes. Points at the hand. Alert tone.
Safe Correction The worker puts down the welder. Grabs the jig on the bench. Places the rebar in the jig, hands free. Both hands on the welder or well clear. The weld proceeds. He finishes the joint, stands up, moves to the next one. Normal. Ambience returns. Welding resumes at a safe distance.

Shot List

  • Wide: Cage assembly area, multiple stations, sparks
  • Medium: Worker picking up rebar, positioning into cage
  • Close-up: Right hand gripping rebar near weld point
  • Extreme close-up: MIG wire feeding from handpiece
  • Close-up: Glove material, thin and insufficient
  • Close-up: Wire contacting/piercing glove (the danger)
  • Close-up: "Eyes on hands" gesture from adjacent worker
  • Medium: Worker grabbing the jig
  • Close-up: Rebar in the jig, hands free
  • Close-up: Weld proceeding, hands well clear
  • Medium: Worker finishing, standing up, moving on

Production Notes

  • Need MIG welder, rebar cage (partially built), and the jig used on site for making cages.
  • Welding PPE and safe distances for crew during filming.
  • The delayed reporting element is worth mentioning in narration. It adds weight to the "it didn't seem like much" attitude that makes these injuries worse.
  • LTI with hospitalisation and surgery. High severity.

Scenario 5

Cleaning a Kibble Jaw by Hand

IR3263 MTI Shepparton Precast

The Incident

A worker was clearing clogged concrete from a skip (kibble) jaw by hand. A second worker, out of sight, operated the skip remotely. The jaw closed on the first worker's right middle finger. Laceration.

The Safe Way

Use a tool (jimmy bar or similar) to scrape the jaw clean. Hands off the jaw entirely. The kibble has no proper lockout point, so tool use and communication are the safe behaviours.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide shot of the concrete batching/pouring area. A skip (kibble) hangs from a crane or sits on a frame. Concrete production in progress. Workers at different stations. Concrete plant ambience. Machinery humming.
The Victim Worker notices the skip jaw is clogged with dried concrete. It's not opening cleanly. Walks up, looks at the blockage. Reaches in with his bare hand and starts picking at the concrete, pulling chunks out. Just clearing it quickly so the job can continue. Scraping. Concrete chipping.
The Mistake Cut to: another worker at the remote control station, not in the first worker's line of sight. Ready to operate the skip. Doesn't know anyone is near the jaw. Close-up: Worker A's fingers inside the jaw opening. Worker B presses the button. The jaw starts to close. Remote control click. Hydraulic movement.
The Accident AI extension: the jaw closes on the fingers. Laceration to right middle finger. Metal jaw closing. Silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "He reached into the jaw with his bare hand. Ten metres away, someone else hit the remote. No call out. No isolation. The jaw closed on his finger."
Rewind Rewind to the moment Worker A reaches into the jaw. Freeze. Worker A's hand inside the jaw, Worker B at the remote out of sight. The disconnect. Rewind sound.
The Gesture Worker A yells out to Worker B. Worker B puts the remote down. They confirm with each other before anything happens near the jaw. [Note: No classic "eyes on hands" gesture in this scenario. The correction is communication and tool use.] Alert tone. Verbal confirmation.
Safe Correction Worker B puts the remote down. They turn off the kibble and isolate it. Worker A grabs a jimmy bar. Using the long-handled tool, he clears the concrete with the remote down and hands well clear. Both workers confirm verbally before operating the skip again. The skip operates. Both walk away. Ambience returns to normal.

Shot List

  • Wide: Concrete batching area, skip/kibble in frame
  • Close-up: Clogged jaw (establish the problem)
  • Medium: Worker A walking up, assessing, reaching in
  • Medium: Worker B at remote control station (separate location, can't see Worker A)
  • Close-up: Fingers inside jaw opening (the danger)
  • Medium: Worker A yelling out to Worker B
  • Medium: Worker B putting remote down
  • Medium: Kibble being turned off and isolated
  • Medium: Worker A with jimmy bar, clearing the jaw with hands clear
  • Wide: Verbal confirmation between workers, skip operating
  • Medium: Both walking away

Production Notes

  • Requires a concrete skip with operable jaw and remote control. Two actors.
  • The split between the two workers, neither can see the other, is the core danger. Film to emphasise the physical separation.
  • Thomas confirmed: the kibble doesn't have a proper lockout point. The safe correction is using a tool (jimmy bar). Don't show lock-out tag-out as the solution here.
  • Filming confirmed at Shepparton Precast.

Scenario 6

Patching a Pipe (Protruding Reo Puncture)

IR740 MTI Shepparton Pipes (afternoon)

The Incident

A worker was patching a concrete pipe spigot end. As the worker ran their hand around the pipe, they punctured their hand on protruding reinforcement they couldn't see.

The Safe Way

Inspect the pipe before patching. Walk around it, look at the full surface before touching it. Look before you touch.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide shot of Shepparton Pipes work area. Concrete pipes in various stages of production. Workers patching, finishing, moving product. Normal production rhythm. Factory ambience. Concrete work sounds.
The Victim A worker walks up to a concrete pipe that needs patching on the spigot end. Picks up patching materials and starts working. Done this before. Routine. Runs his hand around the pipe surface, feeling for imperfections, smoothing the patch. Patching sounds. Hands on concrete.
The Mistake Close-up: the worker's hand slides around the pipe. On the far side, out of his line of sight, a piece of reinforcement protrudes from the concrete. The reo is sharp, sticking out at an angle. His hand moves toward it. He can't see it from where he's standing. Subtle tension tone.
The Accident AI extension: the hand runs into the protruding reo. Puncture wound. Sharp impact. Silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "A piece of reo was sticking out the other side of the pipe. He couldn't see it. He ran his hand straight into it. Ten seconds to walk around and check. That's all it would have taken."
Rewind Rewind to the moment the hand starts moving around the pipe. Freeze. The reo is visible to the camera but not to the worker. His hand is heading straight for it. Rewind sound.
The Gesture A coworker nearby sees the worker about to run his hand blind around the pipe. Taps eyes. Points at the hands. Alert tone.
Safe Correction The worker stops. Steps back. Walks around the pipe and inspects the full surface visually before touching it. Spots the protruding reo. Flags it or removes it. Then proceeds to patch with full awareness of where hazards are. Finishes the job. Walks away. Ambience returns to normal.

Shot List

  • Wide: Pipes work area, concrete pipes, production activity
  • Medium: Worker approaching pipe, picking up patching materials
  • Close-up: Hand running along pipe surface (smooth, routine)
  • Close-up: Protruding reo on the far side of the pipe (visible to camera, not to worker)
  • Close-up: Hand approaching the reo (the danger)
  • Close-up: "Eyes on hands" gesture
  • Medium: Worker walking around the pipe, inspecting visually
  • Close-up: Reo spotted, flagged or removed
  • Medium: Worker patching with full visibility, finishing

Production Notes

  • Film at Shepparton Pipes (afternoon session, after Precast scenarios wrap).
  • Thomas has identified the specific pipe to be used and segregated it on site. The protruding reo is real.
  • This scenario replaced IR909 to rebalance the Pipes workload.
  • The inspection before patching is the key safe behaviour. Simple, takes 10 seconds, prevents the injury entirely.

Scenario 7

Lowering a Mould Onto a Resting Hand

IR3532 Confirm with Thomas Shepparton Precast (morning)

The Incident

Two workers assembling a 900 pit mould. One operated the crane remote, the other guided the outer shell down by hand. While aligning bolts, the crane operator pressed the down button. The second worker's right thumb was caught between the outer shell and the base.

The Safe Way

The person guiding should be the same person on the crane remote. One person, one control, one set of hands. Or: confirm hand position before every down movement.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide shot of the mould assembly area. Overhead crane moving slowly. A 900 pit mould base sitting on the floor. The outer shell is suspended from the crane, ready to be lowered. Two workers approach. One grabs the crane remote, the other positions himself at the mould. Crane motor humming. Factory ambience.
The Victim Worker B stands at the mould base. Reaches up to guide the outer shell as it comes down. Aligning bolt holes, adjusting the shell's position with his hands. His right thumb rests on the edge of the mould base while he peers at the bolt holes. Metal on metal, gentle clinking as the shell sways.
The Mistake Cut to Worker A on the crane remote, a few metres away. Can see the shell but not Worker B's hand position in detail. Thinks they're aligned. Presses down. Cut back to Worker B: the shell lowers. His thumb is on the base. Doesn't move it in time. Crane motor engaging. A descending tone.
The Accident AI extension: the outer shell comes down on the thumb against the base. Crush. Heavy metal contact. Silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "His thumb was resting on the base when the crane brought the shell down on top of it. Two blokes, one set of controls, and nobody watching where the hands were."
Rewind Rewind to the moment Worker B places his thumb on the base. Freeze. Two workers, two roles, no coordinated view of the hand. The disconnect is the danger. Rewind sound.
The Gesture Worker A, before pressing the down button, pauses. Looks directly at Worker B's hands. Sees the thumb on the base. Taps eyes. Points at the thumb. Waits. Alert tone.
Safe Correction Worker B takes his hand off the base. Steps back. Worker A lowers the shell with the remote, or Worker B takes the remote himself. One person guides and controls. The shell lowers cleanly onto the base. Bolts align. They bolt it together. Walk away. Ambience returns to normal. Crane settles.

Shot List

  • Wide: Mould assembly area, crane, mould base on floor, shell suspended
  • Medium: Two workers approaching, splitting roles
  • Close-up: Worker B's thumb on mould base, peering at bolt holes
  • Medium: Worker A at crane remote, separate position (show the physical gap)
  • Close-up: Shell descending toward the thumb (the danger)
  • Close-up: "Eyes on hands" gesture, Worker A pausing and looking at the hand
  • Medium: Worker B stepping back / taking the remote himself
  • Wide: Shell lowering cleanly, both workers finishing the job

Production Notes

  • Need a 900 pit mould (base + outer shell) and overhead crane.
  • The two-worker disconnect is the core visual. Camera should show the separation between them.
  • This may relate to the Queanbeyan incident (10 March 2026, fractured thumb). Confirm with Glenn.
  • Confirm outcome (MTI or LTI) with Thomas.

Scenario 8

Reaching Into a Rebar Cutter

IR2525 LTI (Partial Amputation) Shepparton Precast (morning)

The Incident

A worker was cutting 5 lengths of 12mm rebar on a cutting machine. After a cut, he reached into the machine to remove the cut pieces. The blade activated unexpectedly and amputated a portion of his right ring finger.

The Safe Way

Isolate the machine before reaching in. Power off, lock out, confirm dead. Then clear the pieces.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio
Wider Situation Wide shot of the rebar processing area. Cutting machines, bending machines, stacks of rebar. A worker feeds 12mm rebar into the cutting machine. Makes a cut. Then another. Routine. Efficient. The machine is running smoothly. Machine hum. Rhythmic cutting sounds.
The Victim The worker finishes his fifth cut. The cut pieces are sitting inside the machine. He needs to clear them before the next batch. Pauses. Looks at the pieces. Doesn't bother turning the machine off. It'll only take a second. Reaches in with his right hand. Machine still humming. No change in ambient sound. The machine is still live.
The Mistake Close-up: his fingers enter the cutting zone. The cut pieces are right there. He's almost got them. Close-up: the blade mechanism. Still powered. Something triggers it. An automatic cycle, a sensor, a fault. The blade moves. A mechanical click. The blade engaging.
The Accident AI extension: the blade contacts the right ring finger. Partial amputation of the fingertip. Blade activation. Sharp cut to total silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "He reached into a live machine to grab five pieces of rebar. The blade came down and took part of his finger. Ten seconds to hit the switch. Ten seconds to lock it out. He didn't bother."
Rewind Rewind to the moment his hand enters the machine. Freeze. The machine is running. His hand is inside. No isolation. Rewind sound.
The Gesture A worker at the next machine sees the hand going in. Shouts. Taps eyes. Points at the hand in the machine. Alert tone. Verbal "Oi!"
Safe Correction The worker pulls his hand back. Hits the power-off switch. Applies the lock-out. Confirms the machine is dead. Then reaches in and clears the pieces. Or uses a tool to hook them out. Loads the next batch. Carries on. Machine winding down. Silence. Then normal ambience returns.

Shot List

  • Wide: Rebar processing area, machines running
  • Medium: Worker feeding rebar, making cuts (establish the routine)
  • Close-up: Cut pieces sitting inside the machine
  • Close-up: Worker's hand entering cutting zone (the danger)
  • Close-up: Blade mechanism still powered (the hidden threat)
  • Close-up: "Eyes on hands" gesture, shout and gesture
  • Close-up: Power-off switch, lock-out being applied
  • Medium: Worker clearing pieces from isolated machine
  • Medium: Worker loading next batch, carrying on

Production Notes

Scenario 9

Truck Driver's Hand on the Tray

IR1204 MTI Shepparton Precast (morning)

The Incident

A forklift was placing the last pallet on a truck. The truck driver was leaning on the tray with his hand between a loaded pallet and the side rail. The forklift pushed the pallet in to centre it, crushing the driver's finger.

The Safe Way

Follow the loading bay rules. Stay in the exclusion zone. Hands off the truck while loading is in progress.

Storyboard Beats

BeatVisualAudio

The Victim The truck driver. He's been waiting. Walks up to the side of the truck to check the load. Leans against the tray. The Mistake The forklift approaches. Operator focused on pallet placement. Can see the tray, the gap, but not the driver's hand on the far side. Places the pallet and pushes it in to centre it. The pallet slides toward the already-loaded pallet. The driver's hand is between them, against the rail. Forklift hydraulics. Pallet scraping on the tray.

Tool Clamp (Cubis, Carrum Downs)

IR2785 MTI Carrum Downs, Cubis (separate day, TBD)

The Incident

A worker was aligning tool clamps while lowering the lid of a mould using a crane. Their finger was squashed between the lid and the base of the collar tool.

The Safe Way

When using the crane to lower the lid and close the mould, keep hands clear of the line of fire and pinch point area throughout the descent.

Storyboard Beats

The Victim A worker is at a mould station. The base is in position. The lid needs to come down. He operates the crane to lower the lid. As it descends, he reaches in to align the tool clamps, guiding them into position with his fingers while the lid is still moving. Crane motor. Metal on metal as the lid descends.
The Mistake Close-up: the worker's fingers are on the clamp, between the lid and the base of the collar tool. The lid continues to descend. The gap is closing. His fingers are in the pinch point. He's focused on the alignment, not on where his hands are. Descending tone. The gap narrowing.
The Accident AI extension: the lid meets the base with the finger trapped between. Crush. Metal contact. Silence.
Fade to Black — Narration Black screen. "His fingers were between the lid and the base while the crane was still coming down. The gap closed. His hand was in the pinch point the whole time."
